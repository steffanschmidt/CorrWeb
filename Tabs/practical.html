3D Printing

I have been using 3D printing for the better part of four years on an on/off basis because, I did not have one myself, and I could only do it at Aarhus University. However since I got my own 3D printer, I have made significant progress in understand how to achieve a great end results. The main reason I have used 3D printing a lot is that it is easy to use, cheap and quick to use for making prototypes. It allows for flexible designs and some of the features that is possible to print is not possible or very hard to recreate by machining in one go and vice versa. Even though it is slow for production purposes, then it is great for development or just components small components that are hard to make otherwise and does not need a lot of strength. I have mainly printed with PLA, but also used ABS a couple of times.

Due to the fact I have been using 3D printing for quite some time, despite it being on/off for a long time, I made quite of a lot of different things. This in turns allows me to know the limits of what is possible to print and what is not possible. This obviously depends on the printer in question and a very well optimized printer may push that limit a bit further. In any case my main project with regards to 3D printing has been making small scale corrosion setups that I could use for my experiments to control factors such as height of test object with respect to the water level, aeration and distance between the two metals under galvanic corrosion. However due to their size they did not allow for freedom with regards to the geometric shape or heating. This is what the test setup below attempts to rectify.

Experimental 3D Printed Corrosion Setup

The setup can accommodate different geometric shapes due to the flexible design. Furthermore there are four small and large slots, where two of the latter are reserved for the main axes. These can be opened and closed freely and are intended for aeration, wires, additional materials etc. The sample holders can be moved up and down by using the screws seen in the red and blue holder. There are two small holes at each of the four large slots for possibility of expansion in order to add features and/or equipment for experimental or practical reason. The entire setup was designed to be placed on top of a crystallization bowl as shown and that on top of a magnetic stirrer for heating purposes. I will build the magnetic stirrer myself, since it will be much easier to integrate data collection, feedback loops for temperature control and estimating the corrosion current and much more. Furthermore it will also enable me to implement IoT for real-time monitoring and control. Due to a semi-closed environment, it is possible to simulate marine conditions and how it affects corrosion both in water and air. Lastly there is a window for observation from the top, and since the sample object is in a crystallization bowl, it makes it possible to observe the progress with ones own eye throughout the entire duration of the experiment.

Large Scale Corrosion Setup

As seen above I have been working on designing corrosion setups and making these with 3D print. Most of these have been small scale "kitchen experiments" with the electronics setup, which you can see in the next section. However these are limited both by space both test object wise and the amount of controls I can place on them. This is partly due to the fact that I myself have limited space in my apartment and partly due to a natural evolution of my knowledge in this field. Therefore I have been working on making a large setup that can be seen on the image. This is basically just a box right now, since I am waiting for my electronics to arrive. However the entire idea is creating an inert environment corrosion wise, where I can control a bunch of different parameters for testing corrosion on materials with real-time monitoring. Therefore most of it is made of compact laminate and POM. It consist of a stage (a frame) with spindles in corner for moving the test object up and down. There are two spindles for rotating the test object in the stage plane. All of the spindles are made of POM. This stage will also hold probes for measuring corrosion potentials and estimate currents and other addons (eg. conductivity, water temperature etc.). The bottom of the setup contains two aluminium plates, which will have heater cartridges for heating the water in the setup with thermocouple control. The lid of the box is made of polycarbonate, which makes it possible to observe the experiment at all times. The lid also has three slots, where it is possible to attach extra equipment (eg. a camera for recording).

On the sides of the setup all the instrumentation and controls will be places. The movement will be controlled by step motors with two motors on stage for up/down movement and one for both of the axes in the stage plane. The setup will have 12V, 5V and 3.3V available for different purposes. Data collection and storage will be done through the microcontroller (Teensy 3.5). Overall the setup will be capable of the following:

  • Real-time monitoring of galvanic potential / voltage and current.
  • Possibity to accelerate or stop corrosion.
  • Temperature control in air and water.
  • Air humidity.
  • Rotation of the test object in both of the stage axes.
  • Storage of data on SD card and Cloud.
  • A display showing information and control.
  • Chemically inert and closed environment.
  • Transparent lid for observations and recording of the entire corrosion process.

Other things that can/will be added in time:

  • Conductivity.
  • pH.
Note that this list is not exhaustive and one of the main features of a microcontroller is the ease of expansion, hence I can add things over time as I learn, or if I get suggestions.

As a final note apart from the monitoring then I intend to place a camera on the lid for recording and using image processing to be able to correlate the numbers to the observed effects. This on its own allows for making an empirical model for simulating corrosion based. However in combination with theory it might be possible to make a generalization in order to simulate corrosion based on user inputs and determine lifetimes. At least that is the end goal.
